# AER Paper Writing Skill
## Based on American Economic Review Vol. 115, No. 12 (December 2025) & Vol. 116, No. 1 (January 2026)

---

## Overview

This skill provides comprehensive guidance for writing papers in the style of the *American Economic Review* (AER), the flagship journal of the American Economic Association. The templates, language conventions, and structural norms below are derived directly from published AER papers in the two most recent issues analyzed.

---

## I. Article-Level Format

### 1.1 Header Citation Block

Every AER article begins with a standard citation block at the top of the first page:

```
American Economic Review [YEAR], [VOLUME]([ISSUE]): [START PAGE]–[END PAGE]
https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.[MANUSCRIPT NUMBER]
[START PAGE]
```

**Examples from analyzed issues:**
- `American Economic Review 2025, 115(12): 4105–4136`
- `American Economic Review 2026, 116(1): 1–51`

### 1.2 Title Format

- Titles are **concise and informative**, often using a colon to introduce a subtitle or application context.
- Avoid jargon in the main title; place technical specifics in the subtitle.
- Capitalize all major words.

**Pattern:** `[Theoretical/Conceptual Claim]: [Empirical Context or Application]`

**Examples:**
- "When Product Markets Become Collective Traps: The Case of Social Media"
- "The Opportunity Atlas: Mapping the Childhood Roots of Social Mobility"
- "A Goldilocks Theory of Fiscal Deficits"
- "Downward Rigidity in the Wage for New Hires"
- "Gender Differences in Financial Advice"

### 1.3 Author Line

```
By [First Author Full Name], [Second Author Full Name],
[Third Author Full Name], and [Fourth Author Full Name]*
```

- Use dagger (†) after the title to flag the data/code disclosure footnote.
- Use asterisk (*) after the author list to flag the affiliation footnote.
- List up to four authors on a single line; wrap with line continuation for five or more.

### 1.4 Affiliation Footnote (*)

Standard format for the affiliation footnote:

```
[Last Name]: [University/Institution] and [Research Affiliate, e.g., NBER]
(email: [email]); [Last Name]: [Institution] (email: [email]).
[Coeditor Name] was the coeditor for this article.
We thank [list of anonymous referees and named contributors]...
[Funding acknowledgments, IRB approvals, pre-registration numbers if applicable.]
```

**Key conventions:**
- Name the coeditor who handled the paper.
- Thank "three anonymous referees" (exact phrasing).
- Acknowledge IRB approvals and pre-registrations (AsPredicted, AEA RCT Registry).
- Acknowledge funding agencies in full.

### 1.5 Data/Code Footnote (†)

```
† Go to https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.[ID] to visit the article page for
additional materials and author disclosure statement(s).
```

## II. Abstract

### 2.1 Format Requirements

- **Length:** 100–150 words (strict; most AER abstracts are 120–140 words).
- **No citations** within the abstract.
- **No equations** or mathematical notation.
- **Final element:** JEL classification codes in parentheses on the last line.
- Written in **third person** or impersonal passive voice.
- Describes: (1) research question, (2) approach/data/method, (3) main findings, (4) implications.

### 2.2 JEL Code Format

```
(JEL [Code1], [Code2], [Code3], ...)
```

**Examples:**
- `(JEL D62, D83, D91, L82, Z13)` — Social media welfare paper
- `(JEL G51, I32, I38, J12, R23)` — Opportunity Atlas paper

### 2.3 Abstract Template

```
[1–2 sentences stating the research question and its importance.]
[1–2 sentences describing the empirical strategy, data, or theoretical approach.]
[2–3 sentences summarizing the key findings, including quantitative magnitudes where possible.]
[1 sentence on broader implications or contribution.]
(JEL [codes])
```

### 2.4 Academic Language for Abstracts

**Opening phrases:**
- "We examine the extent to which..."
- "This paper investigates..."
- "We provide the first comprehensive estimates of..."
- "We propose an approach to [measure/account for/characterize]..."
- "We construct [a dataset / a model / a framework] that..."

**Findings phrases:**
- "Our findings indicate that..."
- "We document that..."
- "The results suggest that..."
- "We find that [X] percent of [population] [outcome], while..."
- "Our estimates indicate positive/negative [outcome] of [magnitude]."
- "Experimental and quasi-experimental estimates indicate [X] percent of the variation..."

**Contribution phrases:**
- "Our findings highlight the existence of..."
- "These results challenge the standard [argument/view/assumption] that..."
- "We demonstrate how our [approach/statistics] can be applied to..."

## III. Introduction

### 3.1 Structure (6–8 paragraphs)

AER introductions follow a highly standardized structure:

1. **Paragraph 1 — Hook and Motivation:** Establish the broad phenomenon or stylized fact; cite 3–5 papers in the opening sentence cluster. Convey why the question matters.

2. **Paragraph 2 — The Specific Research Question:** Narrow from the broad motivation to the precise question the paper addresses. Identify the gap in the literature.

3. **Paragraph 3 — Approach and Data:** Describe the identification strategy, dataset, or theoretical framework. Be specific about sample sizes, time periods, and key variation.

4. **Paragraphs 4–5 — Main Results:** Walk through findings in a logical sequence. Lead with the most striking result; provide quantitative magnitudes. Use "We find that..." constructions.

5. **Paragraph 6 — Contribution to Literature:** Explain how the paper relates to 3–5 closely related papers. Distinguish contributions along multiple dimensions (empirical, theoretical, policy).

6. **Paragraph 7 (optional) — Roadmap:** "The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the data. Section III presents..."

### 3.2 Academic Language for Introductions

**Establishing the broad landscape:**
- "Much of [phenomenon] is [descriptive claim]. In many contexts, [mechanism]."
- "Recent experimental and quasi-experimental studies have established that [finding] (Author1 Year; Author2 Year)."
- "The emerging consensus that [X] raises a natural question: [research question]?"
- "Prior research on [topic] has not answered this question because [limitation]."
- "The literature on [topic] has highlighted that [finding] (Author1 Year; Author2 Year; Author3 Year)."

**Introducing the paper's approach:**
- "In this paper, we [propose / construct / examine / study]..."
- "We implement our methodology in [pre-registered / incentivized] [experiments / surveys / analysis] with [N] [units] from [context]."
- "We focus on [X] [prominent / widely used / canonical] [objects], which [reason for selection]."
- "We construct [a publicly available dataset / an atlas / a measure] that provides the first [comprehensive / causal / granular] estimates of [outcome]."

**Presenting main findings:**
- "We start by presenting results on [baseline measure / standard approach]."
- "Our main finding is that [X] percent of [population] [outcome], [qualifying condition]."
- "Average [outcome] is significantly [higher/lower] compared to [benchmark] for both [group A] (p < 0.01) and [group B] (p < 0.01)."
- "The magnitude of [effect] implies [economic interpretation]."
- "This evidence is consistent with [theoretical mechanism]."

**Positioning relative to literature:**
- "Our paper contributes to several strands of the literature."
- "First, we contribute to the literature on [topic] (Author1 Year; Author2 Year)."
- "Second, our paper is related to [adjacent literature] by [relationship]."
- "Our work is most closely related to [Author Year], who [what they did]. We differ from their approach in [key dimension]."
- "In contemporaneous work, [Author Year] [what they do]. Our contribution is complementary in that..."

**Roadmap:**
- "The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows."
- "Section II describes the [theoretical framework / data and institutional background]."
- "Section III presents [the empirical strategy / the model / the experimental design]."
- "Section IV reports [the main results / our findings / the empirical evidence]."
- "Section V discusses [robustness checks / alternative interpretations / mechanisms]."
- "Section VI concludes."

## IV. Section Headings and Structure

### 4.1 Heading Hierarchy

```
I.  Section Title                    (Roman numeral, full caps or title case)
    A.  Subsection Title             (Capital letter, title case)
        Subsubsection Title.—        (Italic or bold, followed by em dash)
```

**Examples from analyzed papers:**
- `I. Introduction`
- `II. A Model of [Topic]`
- `III. Empirical Strategy`
- `IV. Results`
- `V. Robustness and Mechanisms`
- `VI. Conclusion`

Subsections:
- `A. Data Sources`
- `B. Identification Strategy`
- `C. Main Specification`

Sub-subsections (run-in headings):
- `Network Effects.—We next examine...`
- `Measurement Error.—To gauge the importance of noise...`
- `Regret.—To ensure data quality...`

### 4.2 Equation Formatting

- Number equations sequentially in parentheses on the right margin: `(1)`, `(2)`, etc.
- Use standard mathematical notation consistent with the exposition.
- Reference equations as "equation (1)" or "(1)" in the text.

## V. Data Section

### 5.1 Standard Data Section Template

**Paragraph 1 — Data sources:**
```
We use [data source], a [description of dataset] that covers [population/scope]
from [start year] to [end year]. [Data source] was collected/constructed by
[institution] and contains information on [key variables].
```

**Paragraph 2 — Sample construction:**
```
Our primary analysis sample consists of [N] [units], representing approximately
[X] percent of [population of interest]. We focus on [subsample] because [reason].
We restrict the sample to [conditions] and [conditions].
```

**Paragraph 3 — Key variables:**
```
Our primary outcome variable is [outcome], defined as [definition]. We measure
[variable] using [source/method]. Table [N] presents summary statistics for
our main analysis sample.
```

### 5.2 Academic Language for Data Sections

**Describing data sources:**
- "We construct [tract-/firm-/individual-]level statistics using an individual-level panel dataset..."
- "Following [Author Year], we use de-identified data from [source] linked to [other source]..."
- "Our dataset covers virtually the entire [population] from [year] to [year]."
- "We use data from [survey / administrative records / experiment] that contain information on..."

**Describing samples:**
- "Our primary analysis sample consists of [N] [children / firms / workers], approximately [X] percent of [reference population]."
- "We assign [units] to [locations / groups] in proportion to [criterion]."
- "In each [cell / subgroup], we estimate the conditional expectation of [outcome] given [covariates]."
- "We restrict attention to [condition] because [justification]."

**Describing variables:**
- "Our main outcome is [variable], measured as [definition]."
- "We follow the convention in [Author Year] and define [variable] as [definition]."
- "The key explanatory variable is [X], which varies [exogenously / across / over time] due to [source of variation]."

## VI. Empirical Strategy / Theoretical Framework

### 6.1 Empirical Papers — Identification Strategy Template

**Paragraph 1 — Main specification:**
```
To estimate [causal effect / relationship], we use [empirical method:
OLS / IV / RD / DID / event study / randomized experiment]. Our baseline
specification is:

[EQUATION]                                                          (1)

where [Y] denotes [outcome], [X] denotes [key regressor], [controls] are
[description], and [error term] captures [unobservables]. Standard errors
are [clustered at / robust to] [level].
```

**Paragraph 2 — Identification assumption:**
```
The key identification assumption is that [assumption], conditional on
[controls / fixed effects]. [Intuition for why assumption holds.] We provide
supporting evidence for this assumption in [Section / Appendix].
```

**Paragraph 3 — Threats to identification:**
```
One potential concern with our design is that [confound]. To address this,
we [approach]. [Results of robustness check.] Taken together, this evidence
suggests that [conclusion].
```

### 6.2 Academic Language for Empirical Strategy

**Describing the specification:**
- "Our baseline specification regresses [outcome] on [regressor], controlling for [set of controls]."
- "We estimate [the effect of X on Y] using [method], which leverages [source of variation]."
- "To isolate [causal channel], we exploit [quasi-experimental variation / random assignment / discontinuity] in [variable]."
- "Identification relies on the assumption that [treatment/instrument] is as good as randomly assigned, conditional on [controls]."

**Describing instruments:**
- "We instrument for [endogenous variable] using [instrument], which affects [outcome] only through [channel] (exclusion restriction)."
- "The first stage is strong: the F-statistic is [value], well above the conventional threshold of 10."
- "We verify instrument relevance in Table [N], Column [N]."

**Describing fixed effects:**
- "All specifications include [county × year / firm × year / individual] fixed effects to absorb [confounds]."
- "Within-[unit] variation identifies the coefficient of interest."
- "We partial out [individual / spell / match] fixed effects following [Abowd, Kramarz, and Margolis 1999]."

**Describing experimental designs:**
- "The experiment proceeds in [N] main steps."
- "In Step [N], we measure [outcome] using [incentivized / BDM / MPL] mechanism."
- "We pre-registered the analysis at [registry] (no. [ID]) prior to data collection."
- "Participants are [randomly / sequentially] assigned to [treatment / control] conditions."
- "Randomization ensures that treatment is orthogonal to pre-treatment characteristics (p = [value] for joint test)."

## VII. Results Section

### 7.1 Structure

1. **Overview paragraph:** Briefly describe what results will be shown.
2. **Main result tables/figures:** Present coefficient estimates with standard errors; interpret economic magnitudes.
3. **Heterogeneity analysis:** Break down results by subgroup (gender, income, geography).
4. **Robustness checks:** Alternative specifications, samples, or methods.
5. **Mechanism analysis:** Evidence on the channel(s) driving the main result.

### 7.2 Table Format

**Table title format:**
```
Table [N]—[Descriptive Title of What the Table Shows]
```

**Column header format:**
- Dependent variable in header
- Sample restriction in subheader
- Standard errors in parentheses below coefficients

**Notes section (required):**
```
Notes: [Describe what the table shows, sample, and specification.]
[Describe variable definitions.] [Describe control variables included.]
[Specify standard error clustering level and significance star conventions.]
*** p < 0.01, ** p < 0.05, * p < 0.10.
```

### 7.3 Figure Format

**Figure title format:**
```
Figure [N]. [Descriptive Title of What the Figure Shows]
```

**Notes section (required):**
```
Notes: Figure [N] [presents / shows / plots] [description of what is shown].
[Sample description.] [Variable definitions.] [Statistical conventions.]
Error bars represent [95 percent confidence intervals / standard errors].
```

### 7.4 Academic Language for Results

**Presenting the main coefficient:**
- "Column (1) of Table [N] presents [OLS / IV / reduced form] estimates..."
- "The coefficient on [variable] is [estimate] ([se]), significant at the [1/5/10] percent level."
- "The magnitude of [X] implies that a one-standard-deviation increase in [regressor] is associated with a [Y]-standard-deviation [increase/decrease] in [outcome]."
- "[X] percent of [population] [outcome], compared with [Y] percent in [comparison group]."

**Interpreting economic magnitudes:**
- "This estimate implies that [economic interpretation]."
- "To put this in perspective, [benchmark comparison]."
- "The effect size is comparable to [related finding in literature] (Author Year)."
- "This magnitude is economically significant: it amounts to [X] percent of [reference quantity]."

**Presenting heterogeneity:**
- "We next explore heterogeneity by [dimension]."
- "Columns (2)–(4) of Table [N] split the sample by [characteristic]."
- "The effect is concentrated among [subgroup], with [smaller / insignificant] effects for [other subgroup]."
- "Panel [A/B] of Figure [N] illustrates that the results are qualitatively similar across [subgroups]."

**Presenting robustness:**
- "Table [N] explores the sensitivity of our findings to [variation in specification]."
- "The results are robust to [alternative approach / sample restriction / control set]."
- "Column ([N]) replicates Column ([N]) of Table [N] but [modification]. The coefficient remains [similar / statistically significant]."
- "Reassuringly, our estimates are not sensitive to [assumption]."

**Describing mechanisms:**
- "To provide direct evidence on mechanisms, we [approach]."
- "Panel [A] of Figure [N] illustrates that [pattern], which is consistent with [mechanism]."
- "This evidence [corroborates / is inconsistent with / rules out] the hypothesis that [mechanism]."
- "Taken together, these findings suggest that the [main effect] is driven by [mechanism]."

## VIII. Conclusion

### 8.1 Structure (3–4 paragraphs)

1. **Summary:** Restate the research question and key findings without hedging. Use past tense for what the paper did; use present tense for what is now known.
2. **Broader implications:** Connect findings to [policy / theory / the literature]. What does this change about how we think about [topic]?
3. **Limitations and future research:** Acknowledge scope limitations candidly. Point to open questions for future work.
4. **Closing:** Optional brief policy-relevant or forward-looking statement.

### 8.2 Conclusion Template

```
[Restate the traditional approach and its limitation.]
We introduce [novel method / data / framework] to [address this gap] and apply it to
[context]. While [standard measures / prior approaches] suggest [finding A], our
[preferred measure / approach] reveals [finding B]: [quantitative summary of main result].

[Broader implications paragraph.]
Our [empirical evidence / theoretical framework] could help reconcile [seemingly
contradictory findings]. More generally, these findings challenge [standard assumption].
[Policy implication 1.] [Policy implication 2.]

[Limitations paragraph.]
While our evidence shows that [main finding], one limitation for external validity
arises from [limitation]. Moreover, it is an open question whether [open question].
Future work should also examine [research agenda].
```

### 8.3 Academic Language for Conclusions

**Summarizing contributions:**
- "In the traditional assessment of [topic], the emphasis is predominantly on [standard approach]. Such [measures / models] do not accurately reflect [welfare / efficiency / behavior] in settings with [complication]."
- "We introduce a new method to [estimate / characterize / account for] [phenomenon], which we apply to [context] through [approach]."
- "While traditional [measures / models] of [concept] suggest [finding A], the [preferred measure / approach] that accounts for [complication] tells a different story: [finding B]."

**Drawing policy implications:**
- "Our framework highlights [N] important levers for policymakers."
- "First, [regulation / policy instrument] can [counteract / address] [problem]."
- "Second, given that [mechanism], optimal [antitrust / fiscal / monetary] policy may involve [prescription]."
- "The [evidence / estimates] underscore the need for more research on whether [open question]."

**Acknowledging limitations:**
- "While our evidence shows that [finding], one limitation for external validity arises from [limitation]."
- "An important caveat is that [caveat], which may limit the generalizability of our findings to [scope]."
- "Our results are likely [lower / upper] bounds because [reason]."
- "It remains an open question whether [generalization]."

**Pointing to future research:**
- "Future work should examine whether [question]."
- "An important avenue for future research is to [question]."
- "Extending our framework to [setting] is an important direction for future work."
- "Our approach can, in principle, be applied to [other contexts], where [similar forces] may play a role."

## IX. References

### 9.1 AEA Reference Format

The AER uses the American Economic Association reference style. All entries are formatted as follows:

**Journal article:**
```
Last Name, First Name, and First Name Last Name. Year. "Article Title."
Journal Name vol (issue): start–end page.
```

**Book:**
```
Last Name, First Name. Year. Book Title. City: Publisher.
```

**Working paper:**
```
Last Name, First Name, and First Name Last Name. Year. "Paper Title."
[Organization] Working Paper [number].
```

**Online data/code:**
```
Last Name, First Name. Year. Data and Code for: "Paper Title."
American Economic Association; distributed by [repository].
https://doi.org/[DOI].
```

### 9.2 In-Text Citation Format

- **Single author:** Smith (2020) or (Smith 2020)
- **Two authors:** Smith and Jones (2020) or (Smith and Jones 2020)
- **Three or more authors:** Smith et al. (2020) or (Smith et al. 2020)
- **Multiple citations:** (Author1 Year; Author2 Year; Author3 Year) — sorted by author last name alphabetically or chronologically

**Examples from analyzed issues:**
- `(Frank 2005)` — single author
- `(Clark and Oswald 1996)` — two authors
- `(Bursztyn et al. 2018)` — three or more authors
- `(Bottan and Perez-Truglia 2022; Clark and Oswald 1996; Cullen and Perez-Truglia 2022; Luttmer 2005)` — multiple citations

### 9.3 Special Citation Conventions

- **Forthcoming:** Use "forthcoming" instead of year.
- **Mimeo / working paper:** State institution: "NBER Working Paper [N]" or "[University] Mimeo."
- **Datasets:** Cite as a separate entry with author, year, title, and DOI.
- **Pre-registrations:** "AEA RCT Registry. [Month Day]. https://doi.org/..."

## X. Polishing Guide: Academic Language and Style

### 10.1 General AER Style Conventions

| Informal Expression | AER-Style Equivalent |
|---|---|
| "we look at" | "we examine / we investigate / we study" |
| "we show" | "we document / we establish / we demonstrate" |
| "big effect" | "economically significant / large in magnitude" |
| "small effect" | "economically negligible / modest in magnitude" |
| "use data" | "exploit variation in / leverage / use data from" |
| "we check" | "we verify / we validate / we assess the robustness" |
| "due to" | "driven by / attributable to / stemming from" |
| "because of" | "as a result of / owing to / on account of" |
| "we think" | "we interpret this as / the evidence is consistent with" |
| "important" | "salient / consequential / first-order" |
| "shows that" | "indicates that / suggests that / is consistent with" |

### 10.2 Hedging Language (Required for Claims)

AER papers hedge empirical claims carefully:

- **Strong causal claim:** "We estimate that [X] causally [increases/decreases] [Y] by [magnitude]."
- **Suggestive evidence:** "The evidence is consistent with [interpretation]."
- **Descriptive claim:** "We document that [X] is associated with [Y]."
- **Hypothesis test:** "We cannot reject the null that [hypothesis] (p = [value])."
- **Bounding:** "Our estimates likely represent [lower/upper] bounds on [true effect]."

### 10.3 Transition Phrases Between Sections

**Moving to the next result:**
- "We next turn to..."
- "We now examine..."
- "Having established [X], we turn to [Y]."
- "Building on this result, we..."

**Introducing a robustness check:**
- "One potential concern with our baseline specification is [concern]. To address this, [approach]."
- "An alternative interpretation of these findings is [alternative]. To rule this out, we [test]."
- "To ensure that our results are not driven by [concern], we [modification]."

**Summarizing a subsection:**
- "Taken together, these findings suggest that..."
- "In sum, the evidence in this subsection [supports/is consistent with/challenges]..."
- "Overall, the results of this section indicate that..."

### 10.4 Quantitative Reporting Conventions

- Always report **both** the point estimate and the standard error (in parentheses).
- Report p-values for key comparisons: `(p < 0.01)`, `(p = 0.03)`.
- Use "percent" (not "%") in the text; use "%" in tables.
- Express magnitudes in interpretable units when possible.
- Use "approximately" or "roughly" when rounding.
- Express fractions as "X percent" not "X%": "Ninety-two percent of TikTok users..."
- Capitalize "Figure" and "Table" when referring to specific items: "Figure 5 shows..."

### 10.5 Section-Specific Language Polishing

#### Abstract Polishing Checklist
- [ ] 100–150 words?
- [ ] No citations?
- [ ] Ends with JEL codes?
- [ ] States research question in first sentence?
- [ ] Reports at least one quantitative finding?
- [ ] Uses precise, formal vocabulary?

#### Introduction Polishing Checklist
- [ ] Opens with a broad, compelling observation?
- [ ] Motivates the specific research question by paragraph 2?
- [ ] Describes approach, data, and key variation by paragraph 3?
- [ ] Presents main findings with specific numbers?
- [ ] Situates contribution in literature?
- [ ] Includes roadmap paragraph?

#### Results Polishing Checklist
- [ ] All tables have numbered titles and detailed notes?
- [ ] All figures have numbered titles and detailed notes?
- [ ] Point estimates reported with standard errors?
- [ ] Economic magnitudes interpreted?
- [ ] Robustness checks presented?
- [ ] Heterogeneity analysis included?

#### Conclusion Polishing Checklist
- [ ] Restates main finding without hedging?
- [ ] Draws policy or broader implications?
- [ ] Honestly acknowledges limitations?
- [ ] Points to future research agenda?
- [ ] No new results introduced?

## XI. Common AER Paper Types and Specific Guidance

### 11.1 Experimental Papers

**Key elements:**
- Pre-registration (AsPredicted or AEA RCT Registry) — cite in footnote.
- IRB approval — cite in footnote.
- Detailed description of the incentivized mechanism (BDM, MPL, randomization).
- Balance table (Table 1) verifying randomization.
- CONSORT-style description of sample attrition.
- Pre-specified vs. exploratory analysis clearly distinguished.

**Language:**
- "The experiment was pre-registered at [registry] (no. [ID]) prior to data collection."
- "The research described in this article was approved by the [Institution] Institutional Review Board (IRB [number])."
- "We implement our design using an incentivized [BDM / MPL] mechanism."
- "Participants were randomly assigned to [condition], with randomization stratified by [variable]."

### 11.2 Quasi-Experimental / Reduced-Form Papers

**Key elements:**
- Clear statement of identifying variation.
- Parallel trends test (for DID) or McCrary density test (for RD).
- Event study plot showing pre-trends.
- Placebo tests.
- Instrumental variable: first-stage F-statistic, exclusion restriction argument.

**Language:**
- "Our identification strategy exploits [variation] that is plausibly exogenous to [outcome] conditional on [controls]."
- "The key assumption is that [treatment] is as good as randomly assigned within [comparison unit]."
- "Figure [N] presents event study estimates, which confirm parallel pre-trends (joint p = [value])."

### 11.3 Structural / Theory Papers

**Key elements:**
- Formally stated model with numbered assumptions and propositions.
- Proof sketches in text; full proofs in appendix.
- Calibration or estimation section connecting model to data.
- Counterfactual simulations.

**Language:**
- "Assumption [N] (Label). [Formal statement of assumption.]"
- "Proposition [N]. [Formal statement of result.] Proof. See Appendix [N]."
- "We calibrate the model to match [moments] from [data]. Table [N] reports the calibrated parameter values."

---

## XII. Appendix and Online Appendix

### 12.1 Structure

- **Main appendix:** Proofs, additional tables, robustness checks — included in the published paper.
- **Supplemental appendix / online appendix:** Detailed experimental instructions, data construction details, additional figures — available online only.

### 12.2 Notation Conventions

- Reference as "Supplemental Appendix [Letter]" (e.g., "Supplemental Appendix A") for online materials.
- Reference as "Appendix [Letter/N]" for within-paper materials.
- Tables in appendix: "Supplemental Appendix Table A[N]" or "Online Appendix Table [A-N]."
- Figures in appendix: "Supplemental Appendix Figure A[N]."
